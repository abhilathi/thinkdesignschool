From IC Designer to Design Lead:
What Actually Changes

The promotion looks like a reward. It's actually a completely different job. Here's what nobody tells you before you make the move — and what to do about it.

By Abhilash S
7 min read
Career · Leadership · Design
The Moment It Hits You

The first week as a lead felt like a demotion

I remember my first week as a design lead clearly. I had fewer screens to design. More meetings. A team that needed direction I wasn't sure how to give. And a vague, uncomfortable feeling that I wasn't actually doing any real work.

That feeling is common. And it's a signal — not that you made a mistake, but that you haven't yet understood what the new job actually is.

As an IC (individual contributor), your job is to produce excellent design work. As a lead, your job is to create the conditions for excellent design work to happen — through other people, and without always being the one holding the pen.

"The hardest part of becoming a design lead isn't learning new skills. It's letting go of the old ones as your primary identity."

IC vs Lead

The same words mean different things now

The language of design doesn't change. But what each word requires of you does.

As an IC designer
You make design decisions
You own the output
You advocate for the user
You get feedback
You finish the project
Your success = great work shipped
As a design lead
You create frameworks for others to decide
You own the team's output
You teach others to advocate
You give feedback — well
You help others finish
Your success = others doing great work

The First 90 Days

What good leads actually do in their first three months

MONTH 1
Listen more than you speak. Understand how the team works, what slows them down, and what they're proud of. Changing things before you understand them is the fastest way to lose trust.
MONTH 2
Remove one friction. Find the single biggest thing that slows the team down — a process, a meeting, a handoff gap — and fix it. Small, visible improvements build credibility faster than grand visions.
MONTH 3
Define what good looks like. Not by telling people what to do — but by facilitating a conversation where the team defines the quality bar together. People follow standards they helped create.
The Honest Truth

Not every great designer should become a lead

This is the thing nobody says out loud. Leadership is a different craft. Some designers are at their best and happiest as individual contributors — doing deep, focused craft work — and that is not a lesser path.

The IC track can go very deep. Principal designer. Staff designer. Distinguished designer. These roles carry serious influence without requiring you to manage people or run team rituals.

🎨 IC PATH IS RIGHT IF
You get energy from doing the work. You want to go deep on craft. Meetings drain you. Your best days are designing, not directing.
🧭 LEAD PATH IS RIGHT IF
You get energy from enabling others. You care about team culture. You want to shape direction, not just execute it. You find teaching more satisfying than making.

The question isn't which path is better. The question is which path is yours.

"The best design leads I know didn't choose leadership because it was the next step. They chose it because they genuinely wanted to help other designers grow."
