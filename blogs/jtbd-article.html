The Opening Thought

My nephew taught me this without knowing it

Last monsoon, my 8-year-old nephew was excited when we bought him a new school bag. Not because of the bag. Because it had a Doraemon print on it.

He didn't care about the zips, the compartments, or the padding. He cared about what his classmates would say when he walked in on Monday morning with Doraemon on his back.

We thought we were buying a school bag. He was buying a cool moment in front of his friends.

"That tiny difference — between what we think people buy and what they actually buy — is the entire idea of Jobs To Be Done."

The product is just the vehicle. The destination is always a feeling.

The Samosa Theory

Every job has 3 layers

A samosa has an outer shell, a spiced filling, and a core. Every job people want done has the exact same structure. Tap each layer to see what's really inside.

"I need to buy a new phone. My old one is slow and keeps crashing."

Practical. Obvious. This is what most businesses build for — and stop at.

Most businesses only see the outer layer. The best ones go all the way to the core.

Old vs New Thinking

The question that changes everything

The same product. Two completely different ways of understanding the person holding it.

❌ OLD THINKING
"We sell a ceiling fan. 5 blades. 3 speeds. White and brown."
✅ JTBD THINKING
"People hire us so their family can sleep peacefully on a hot night — and not wake up irritable."
❌ OLD THINKING
"We sell tiffin delivery. Home-cooked meals. On-time. Affordable."
✅ JTBD THINKING
"People hire us to feel cared for — 500 kilometres away from the people who love them."

One is about the object. The other is about the life of the human being holding it. That is the only difference — and it changes everything about how you build, sell, and market.

The Big Idea

The product is always just the vehicle

The umbrella buyer wants to move freely without weather making decisions for them. The diary buyer wants to feel less alone with their thoughts. The birthday cake buyer wants to create a memory someone keeps for years.

When you build, sell, or market something — start with the life your customer is actually living. Not the product you're selling.

"That gap between your customer's life now and their life after — that is the job. Fill that gap, and everything else follows."

This is what the world's best companies do. They're not smarter. They just ask a different question.

Not: "How do I make my product better?"
But: "What is the life my customer is trying to live?"
